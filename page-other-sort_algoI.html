What Sorting Alogrithm is and Why Time-complexity Matters

Comparison vs Non-comparison

Sorting Algorithms are used to sort a list of elements in certain orders, i.e. numerical or alphanumerical. Some of these algorithms compare elements, and others don't. Ones that do comparison are also called comparison sort algorithms. Merge sort, insertion sort and bubble sort fall into the category.

Any comparison sort algorithm has a lower bound of O(nLogn) for time-complexity, i.e. the time required for sorting n elements cannot grow slower than O(nLogn).

Non-comparison sort algorithms, such as Counting sort, or Radix sort, in comparison, orders elements based on the knowledge or assumption already established about the data. For example, Counting sort utilizes the known range of elements in the data and takes O(n) time-complexity.

Time Complexity

In computer science, the time-complexity of an algorithm is the amount of time that it takes to complete a certain task. Time-complexity is commonly expressed using "Big O Notation", like O(n) - meaning linear growth, or O(n2) - polynomial growth.

An Example of O(n) would be counting a number of n people in a room. You only have to count every one once. Even when there are twice as many people as before(2n), the amount of time that takes to count 2n number of people is exactly twice the case of n people, i.e. the complexity growth in linear manner.



In other words, making them handshake one another takes O(n2) complexity. 4 people requires 6 (3 +2 +1) handshakes, 5 people 10 handshakes (4 +3 +2 +1). The exact formula for this problem would be n(n-1)/2, but we usually take the fastest-growing term tg and write O(tg).

Time-complexity of algorithms, hence, do matter as they are the indicator of how efficient an algorithm is. An optimal algorithm solves a problem with the least amount of time while an non-optimal algorithm take more. Reference:  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_complexity
